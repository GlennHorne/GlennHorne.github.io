Project Idea

Overview

My proposed project is an animated adventure in which Loener, an erudite student, makes his way through a memory palace that has been specifically designed for the learning of 50 English verbs. The target group of the project is Australian adults who may have a problem with English literacy and with problem solving in technology-rich environments. The project will develop basic interactive skills using either a desktop or laptop computer and at the same time develop the user’s literacy capacity. The project is essentially a computer-based programme, and it is not intended for distribution as an app for other devices.

Motivation

Some 12.6% of adults in Australia attain only Level 1 or below in literacy proficiency. That is, these adults can read brief texts on familiar topics and locate a single piece of specific information identical in form to information in the question or directive. Some 38.1% score at or below Level 1 in problem solving in technology rich environments, i.e. these adults can only use widely available and familiar technology applications to solve basic problems involving few steps, simple reasoning and little or no navigation across applications (www.oecd.org, 2013). Therefore, this project will be useful in addressing both of the above problems.

www.oecd.org. (2013). OECD Skills Surveys. [online] Available at: https://www.oecd.org/skills/piaac/Country%20note%20-%20Australia_final.pdf [Accessed 6 Dec. 2019].

Description

It is proposed that initially the usage of 50 common English verbs will be explored. Later developments could include less common verbs, and thus the programme could mirror the style of old-school computer and video games in that it would have easy, medium, and hard difficulty levels.

To this end, the character, Loener, as he makes his way through the memory palace, will stop at specific locations and perform an action demonstrating the use of the verb to be explored. The user will be prompted to answer the question, “Where is Loener and what is he doing?”, and thus the user will be called upon to recall from memory the word describing what the action may be that is associated with the animation on the computer screen in front of her or him.

An alternative approach to the course of events could involve Loener simply stop at the appropriate location and not perform any action at all. The user would in this approach be presented with the image of a playing card from a standard deck of cards and be prompted to recall to memory a verb assigned to that card. This approach would involve the Major method of constructing a word for memorization. The Major method involves the use of 10 distinct sounds that are associated with the numbers zero to nine. The sound “ta” or “da” is associated with the number one. Utilizing another method, the suits of a deck of cards are assigned the following numbers:

  • Spades 11 – 23
  • Diamonds 31 – 43
  • Clubs 51 – 63
  • Hearts 81 - 93

Therefore, combining the two methods, the number representing the ace of spades is 11, and an example of the associated sound would be “ta-ta”, “ta-da”, “da-ta”, or “da-da”. A word made up of these sounds could be “tidy” and thus Loener could be at the sublocation, “shelves”, of the “walk-in robe” of the memory palace tidying his clothes. Therefore, in response to the question, “Where is Loener and what is he doing?”, the user would enter, “He is in the walk-in robe, tidying his clothes”.

Other features could include the option to create a memory palace of one’s own, such as the current or a former residence of the user, and the ability to programme Loener to stop at the location desired where a card could also be placed. (Re-animation of Loener would perhaps be, at this stage of development of the programme, a little too difficult to introduce.) The memory palace could perhaps be even entirely of the user’s imagination!

The default memory palace would be that of a two-story townhouse with eleven rooms and sub-locations within these rooms at which Loener will stop. The sublocations would be similar to the example given above, i.e. the shelves of the walk-in robe. The number of words, 50, has been chosen because there are 52 cards in a standard deck of playing cards, and this would suffice for the programme’s purpose.
